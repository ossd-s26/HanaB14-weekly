## Reaserch ideas about open …
On Monday’s class, we explored open source ideas in different areas, not just software.

One example from the instructor was Open Seeds Initiative, which focuses on making seeds open and shareable. Instead of companies controlling seeds, people can grow, modify, and share them freely. This shows that open source can also apply to biology and agriculture.

Another example from a classmate was OpenStax, which provides free and low-cost textbooks. What I found interesting is that these books can be remixed and edited by teachers. Compared to traditional textbooks, this gives more flexibility and reduces cost for students.
<!--more-->

In my own research, I looked at OpenClaw, an open-source AI agent system. It allows users to build assistants and share skills through a platform called ClawHub. Unlike the other two examples, OpenClaw is closer to software, but it still goes beyond just code because people can contribute reusable “skills” instead of only contributing source code.

---
## Reflection
Looking at these three examples together, I realized that open source is not only about coding. It is really about sharing control and lowering barriers, which changed how I understand it:

- Open Seeds → freedom in physical resources
- OpenStax → access to education
- OpenClaw → collaboration in AI systems

I also started to notice that this idea of sharing knowledge, transparency, and collaboration actually appears in many parts of daily life, not just in technology.

This made me rethink open source as a broader idea: it is about giving people the right to use, modify, and share, no matter the field.

## My Contribution in Practice
Last week, I tried a new type of contribution for AFFiNE, which also connects to my understanding of open source beyond just web development.

This experience helped me understand more about cross-platform development. For example, I learned that I need to run yarn render first and then start Electron. If not in this order, the Electron app may just show a blank screen, which is different from normal web development.

Trying Electron made me realize that being a “full stack engineer” is not just about frontend + backend. It also includes understanding different environments like desktop apps.

Compared to my previous web fixes, this felt more challenging because:
- debugging is harder
- environment setup is more complex
- behavior is slightly different from browser

But I also feel this is more valuable. It pushes me out of my comfort zone.

Next, I want to try contributing to **mobile-related issues**, so I can continue exploring different platforms and become more flexible as a developer.
