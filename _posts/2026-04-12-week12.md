## A Small Memory from High School

Before I formally learned what open source meant, I think I had already used its idea in a small way.

When I was in high school, I was the class monitor and often organized class meetings. Every time I needed to prepare slides, I usually searched online for free slide templates. These websites offered many beautiful layouts for free, and I only needed to replace the text, add related photos, and adjust the design based on the meeting topic.

At that time, I did not think about this as “open source.” I simply felt that it saved me a lot of time. I did not need to spend hours rebuilding the same title pages, section layouts, or color styles from zero. Instead, I could focus more on what really mattered: the story of the meeting, the emotions I wanted to create, and how to make the slides feel more engaging for my classmates.

Looking back, I now realize this was my earliest understanding of the open-source mindset: **shared structure creates more room for creativity.**

## Connecting It to This Week’s OSSD Class

This week’s OSSD class discussion about industry use of open source made this idea much clearer for me.

One point from the slides that stood out was the idea of the **commodity layer vs. differentiation layer**. Companies often collaborate on the common “plumbing” parts of software, such as operating systems, protocols, or shared tools, and then compete on the unique user experience and product creativity.

This immediately reminded me of both my old slide templates and my recent web design class.

---

## Bootstrap Feels Like the Same Idea

Recently, we started learning Bootstrap in web design. Bootstrap gives us ready-made grid systems, buttons, navigation bars, and responsive layouts.

Just like the free slide templates I used before, it removes repeated structural work. I no longer need to spend all my time rewriting the same layout logic or media queries. Instead, I can focus more on page atmosphere, visual storytelling, and how users move through the website.

This made me realize that this is exactly why industries use open source. The value is not only that it is “free,” but that it reduces repeated engineering work and lets teams spend more time on what makes their product different.

---

## How This Connects to My Own OSS Work

I also saw a connection to my own open-source UI contributions.

In the UI fixes and small feature improvements I worked on, the shared infrastructure already existed. My role was not to rebuild the whole system, but to improve clarity, usability, and user experience. That is where unique value is created.

This idea now feels very natural to me: use shared foundations for repeated work, and spend original effort on design, user flow, and better experiences.

---

## Final Reflection

This class changed how I think about software and design.

Good builders do not always start from zero. Sometimes the better skill is knowing which layers should be shared and which parts deserve your original creativity.

From free slide templates in high school, to Bootstrap in web design, to UI contributions in open source today, I feel I am gradually understanding the same core idea in deeper ways:

**shared foundations help people create better and more meaningful experiences.**
