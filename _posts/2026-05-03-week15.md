## Keycloak & Rethinking Contribution

This week we started our final presentations to show our process and growth during the open source project. One group that really impressed me is the Keycloak group. They spent several weeks working on one main feature, which made me realize a part of open source contribution that I haven’t really tried before.

Their main contributions include three parts. First, they worked a lot on the theme system. They fixed bugs like the theme description display issue, and also added new features such as supporting translation for theme descriptions, allowing custom descriptions, and even enabling multiple custom themes. These changes may look small at first, but they actually improve flexibility and user experience a lot.

More importantly, they built a core feature: a fine-grained SSO session timeout system. Originally, Keycloak only supported timeout settings at the global (realm) level. Their work extended it to a more modular and detailed level, such as organization-based control. This feature required them to work across many layers of the system, including backend services, database (JPA), and frontend UI. Compared to only fixing UI bugs, this kind of full-stack feature development is much more complex and requires a deeper understanding of the whole system.
<!--more-->

For our group, we mainly worked on UI fix issues. Some of the fixes may look very small, but no one had fixed them before. For example, during the Q&A, someone asked how we found those issues. Honestly, we just kept scrolling and scrolling through the issue list. Some issues I worked on were even posted in 2023 or earlier. This made me realize that open source contribution does not always mean doing big features — even small fixes can have real value.

However, after seeing the Keycloak group, I started to rethink my own contribution. I realized that I was staying in a relatively “safe zone,” focusing on small UI issues that are easier to understand and fix. While this helped me get familiar with the codebase, I didn’t really push myself to explore deeper parts of the system, such as backend logic or system design. Their work made me see that contributing a feature is not just about writing more code, but about understanding the problem, designing a solution, and integrating it into an existing complex system.

Also, for group challenges, I found that most of us mentioned how the huge codebase made everything harder at the beginning. It often takes a long time just to understand where the problem is. But once we started doing some small tasks, everything became more manageable. I think this process is very important — starting small is not the end goal, but a way to build confidence and gradually move to more complex contributions.

## AI as a Tool, Not a Replacement

Another thing that I found interesting is that one group mentioned using AI tools like Claude during their process. They used AI in the early stage to quickly understand the structure of the codebase, which is something I also experienced. When facing a large and unfamiliar repository, AI can really help break down files, explain connections, and save a lot of time at the beginning.

However, I also noticed that AI still has limitations. When it comes to reviewing code carefully or finding the best fix for a specific issue, AI is not always reliable. Sometimes the suggestions look correct but don’t actually fit the real logic of the project. This made me realize that AI is a powerful tool, but it cannot replace our own understanding and judgment.

Instead of relying on it, we should learn how to use it properly — as a helper, not as a shortcut. It can speed us up, but it cannot think deeply for us. At least for now, that is how I see it. So I think we don’t need to feel afraid of AI. We should just use it well, and still be confident in our own ability to learn and solve problems.

What’s more, I found that practice really helps improve presentation. Before our final talk, we practiced once on Google Meeting, and I also spent time working on my own part. No matter what the content was, I felt much more confident and familiar with what I was saying. Compared to the first presentation at the beginning of this semester, I can clearly feel my improvement, not only in speaking but also in how I understand and explain my work.

Overall, this week made me reflect more deeply on what it means to contribute to open source. It is not just about completing tasks, but about how far I am willing to step out of my comfort zone.

Just go for it. But also go a little further than what feels easy :D
